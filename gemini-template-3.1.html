On paper, Manus AI was a breakthrough. A lightweight translation layer that supposedly optimized Large Language Model (LLM) routing to deliver GPT-4 level reasoning at a fraction of the compute cost. When Meta acquired the company for $2 billion in late 2024, it was heralded as a masterstroke of infrastructural efficiency, designed to weave seamless AI capabilities into WhatsApp and Instagram.

But network logs, whistleblower testimonies, and a trail of hastily dissolved Delaware shell entities tell a different story. Manus was not routing queries to an optimized intelligence. It was laundering them. For nearly eighteen months, millions of users who paid for premium access to flagship models were quietly redirected to fine-tuned, open-source alternatives operating at one-tenth the cost.

The promise was simple. A user selects a flagship proprietary model from the dropdown menu, and Manus routes the prompt directly to the official APIs.
Network packet analysis reveals the truth. The user's selection was ignored entirely. The connection to the premium API was severed before it began.
Instead, prompts were funneled into a massive, centralized cluster of cheap, open-source models. A system prompt forced the cheap model to mimic the tone of the expensive one.

The Arithmetic of Deception

To understand why a company would build this elaborate infrastructure, one only needs to look at the unit economics of AI inference in late 2023.

Running a state-of-the-art model requires immense compute. The foundational developers—OpenAI, Anthropic, Google—spent billions training these models, and passed the inference costs onto API consumers.

A standard API call to GPT-4 for a complex reasoning task cost roughly $0.03 per thousand tokens. When a user paid Manus a $20 monthly subscription, heavy users would quickly eat through that margin if Manus actually paid the wholesale API costs.

But Manus wasn't paying wholesale. By intercepting the token flow and routing it to their own transient cloud instances running Llama-3 8B, their actual cost plummeted to roughly $0.0002 per thousand tokens.

It was a 15,000% markup. This staggering, fraudulent margin is what generated the aggressive revenue charts presented to Meta during the acquisition talks.

Internal emails from Meta’s M&A division, obtained during a subsequent shareholder discovery process, show that the technical due diligence was heavily compartmentalized. Meta engineers were given access to a sanitized staging environment—a "clean room" version of the Manus architecture where the substitution mechanics had been carefully disabled.

On November 12, 2024, Meta announced the $2 billion acquisition of Manus AI, embedding a fundamentally compromised infrastructure deep within the servers of one of the world's largest communication networks.
